## The 30-second version

Reranking is the second pass in a two-stage retrieval funnel: a fast bi-encoder (the embedding model behind vector search) casts a wide net over the whole corpus in milliseconds, then a slower, sharper cross-encoder re-scores just that shortlist by reading the query and each candidate *together*. The bi-encoder embeds query and document separately and compares fixed vectors afterward — cheap, but blind to how specific words in the query interact with specific words in a document. The cross-encoder feeds both into one model at once, so it catches relevance a bi-encoder's vector-distance math structurally cannot. The catch is cost: cross-encoders run per-candidate, not once, so you only ever apply one to a shortlist of tens of candidates, never the full corpus. Skip it when your latency budget is under about 100ms total, or when first-stage results are already cleanly separated.

## The analogy

A bi-encoder is a resume-screening algorithm that scores resumes with no job posting in front of it; a cross-encoder is the hiring manager who actually reads each shortlisted resume against the specific role.

An applicant-tracking system pre-scores every resume in the pile against a generic profile — years of experience, keyword density, degree field — and it does this *before* anyone has even opened a specific req. It's fast precisely because it never compares a resume to a job description directly; each resume gets one fixed score, computed once, reusable across every future search. That's the bi-encoder: query and document encoded independently, compared only as an afterthought, through simple vector math.

Run that scored pile through a search for "Senior Backend Engineer, payments experience required," and the algorithm returns its top 50 by generic score — a reasonable, if occasionally confused, shortlist. Now a hiring manager sits down with those 50 resumes and the actual job req open beside each one. She isn't scoring resumes in a vacuum anymore — she's asking, for *this* resume against *this* req, specifically: does "led checkout infrastructure migration" actually mean payments experience? A keyword-matching pre-filter might miss that; a human reading both documents together catches it immediately. That joint reading — query and candidate considered side by side, not independently — is exactly what a cross-encoder does with attention layers instead of human judgment.

The catch is obvious once you say it aloud: the hiring manager cannot personally read all 10,000 resumes in the pile against the req — that doesn't scale. She can absolutely read the pre-filtered 50. That divide-and-conquer — cheap net first, expensive judgment second, only on the shortlist — is the entire architecture of production retrieval.

| Hiring analogy | Technical element |
|---|---|
| Pre-scoring every resume with no job req in view | Bi-encoder: encodes query and document independently |
| The pre-computed, reusable per-resume score | A stored embedding vector |
| ATS returns top 50 by generic score | First-stage retrieval (vector search) |
| Hiring manager reading the req and resume together | Cross-encoder: joint query-document encoding |
| Catching "led checkout migration" = payments experience | Attention over query-document interaction a bi-encoder can't see |
| She can't read all 10,000 resumes personally | Cross-encoders don't scale to the full corpus |
| She can absolutely read the pre-filtered 50 | Reranking a small shortlist (typically top 20-100) |

## How it actually works

![A corpus of 10 million chunks narrows through fast bi-encoder retrieval to a top 50, then through slow cross-encoder reranking to a top 5, before reaching the LLM prompt; a note below explains bi-encoders score query and doc separately while cross-encoders score them together](diagrams/reranking--two-stage-funnel.svg)

Follow the funnel left to right — it only ever narrows. The corpus starts at millions of chunks. **Bi-encoder retrieval** (the [vector database](./vector-databases.mdx) doing an ANN search) embeds the query, compares it against every pre-computed document vector, and returns a top 50 in single-digit-to-low-double-digit milliseconds — fast because each document's vector was computed once, offline, and comparing vectors is cheap arithmetic. **Cross-encoder reranking** then takes only those 50 and, for each one, feeds `[query, candidate]` into a single model that jointly attends over both texts, producing a relevance score per pair. That's an O(n) operation per query — n forward passes, one per candidate — which is exactly why it never runs against the full corpus: at 10 million chunks, n forward passes would take unusable time. The survivors — typically the top 5 to 10 by cross-encoder score — are what actually reach the LLM's prompt.

The neutral note under the funnel names the structural reason cross-encoders win on precision: a bi-encoder computes `embed(query)` and `embed(doc)` in complete isolation and only compares the two fixed vectors afterward, so it can never represent "this specific query term changes how I should read that specific document phrase." A cross-encoder processes both texts as one input, letting attention layers directly relate query tokens to document tokens — which is also why cross-encoders substantially outperform on the "lost in the middle" problem: they're trained to weigh relevant content regardless of where it sits in the passage, not just how close its overall vector lands to the query's.

## A concrete example

An HR help-desk bot retrieves for the query "how do I carry over unused vacation days into next year."

- **Bi-encoder scores (cosine similarity), top 3 candidates:** a passage titled "Time off policy overview" scores 0.74 and ranks #1; a passage that literally says "Up to 5 unused PTO days roll over into Q1 of the following year" scores only 0.71 and ranks #2 — the bi-encoder's fixed vectors put a generic overview ahead of the one passage that actually answers the question, because vector distance doesn't capture that "carry over" and "roll over" are the operative match here, not the shared vocabulary around "policy."
- **Cross-encoder scores on the same three, reading query and passage jointly:** the "roll over into Q1" passage jumps to 0.90 and ranks #1; the generic overview drops to 0.66. The reranker caught what cosine similarity missed.
- **Latency budget:** first-stage retrieval runs in roughly 50–100ms; a cross-encoder reranking 50 candidates typically adds 100–200ms more, for a combined 150–300ms total — versus 50–100ms with no reranking at all.
- **Quality delta:** it's common to see NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain, a standard ranking-quality metric) move from roughly 0.65 without reranking to roughly 0.78 with it — a meaningful jump for a few hundred milliseconds.
- **Sweet spot for candidate count:** retrieving 20 candidates and reranking them often lands around NDCG 0.72 at ~120ms; retrieving 50 and reranking often reaches ~0.76 at ~180ms; pushing to 100 candidates typically adds only marginal quality (~0.77) for a much heavier ~280ms — the elbow of diminishing returns usually sits around K = 50.

## The tradeoffs that matter

| Approach | Precision | Latency added | Cost | Best fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| No reranking | Lower (raw bi-encoder ranking) | None | Lowest | Tight latency budgets (<100ms), simple single-term queries |
| Cross-encoder reranking | High | +100-200ms typical | Compute per candidate (self-host) or per-call API fee | Most production RAG serving customer-facing answers |
| LLM-based (listwise) reranking | Highest, handles nuance | +1-3s | 10-100x a cross-encoder | Low-volume, high-stakes domains (legal, medical) |
| Distilled small-model reranking | Near cross-encoder quality | <10ms | Low — CPU-feasible | Latency-critical paths that still want a precision pass |

The honest framing: reranking's cost scales with how many candidates you feed it, not with corpus size — that's exactly why the two-stage design works at all. The real decision is where to spend a fixed latency budget: interviewers and production teams both converge on "retrieve cheaply, rerank the shortlist," because reranking 50 candidates delivers most of the achievable quality gain, and reranking 200 rarely earns its extra latency.

## Where people go wrong

1. **Reranking the entire corpus.** Cross-encoders are O(n) per query — running one against millions of documents is a latency and cost disaster. Always narrow with cheap retrieval first.
2. **Skipping reranking by default "for speed."** Many teams ship bare vector search and accept a real, measurable precision loss (~0.65 vs. ~0.78 NDCG in typical benchmarks) without ever testing whether their latency budget could absorb a reranker.
3. **Over-retrieving before reranking with no evidence it helps.** Past roughly K=50-100 candidates, quality gains flatten while latency keeps climbing — more isn't automatically better.
4. **Treating LLM-based reranking as a drop-in reranker replacement.** It's 10-100x the cost and seconds slower than a cross-encoder; reserve it for low-volume, high-stakes queries, not the default path.
5. **Ignoring the reranker in evaluation.** Teams measure retrieval recall and stop — but the reranker changes final ranking and often final precision more than the retriever does. Evaluate the pipeline's output, not just the first stage's.

## The interview lens

Interviewers use reranking to test whether you understand precision-versus-recall tradeoffs operationally, not just conceptually.

A strong sound bite: *"First-stage retrieval optimizes for recall over the whole corpus cheaply; reranking optimizes for precision on a small shortlist expensively — and the entire two-stage design exists because you can't afford a cross-encoder's per-document cost at corpus scale, but you can absolutely afford it on fifty candidates."*

Likely follow-ups:

- Why can't you just use a cross-encoder for first-stage retrieval and skip the bi-encoder entirely?
- How do you decide how many candidates to retrieve before reranking?
- When would you reach for LLM-based or listwise reranking instead of a standard cross-encoder?
